# Neustaedter & Sengers (2012) - Autobiographical Design in HCI Research
## STAR-Structured Summary

**Citation:** Neustaedter, C., & Sengers, P. (2012). Autobiographical design in HCI research: Designing and learning through use-it-yourself. *Proceedings of the Designing Interactive Systems Conference (DIS '12)*, 514-523.

---

## S (Situation): Research Context and Problem

### The Hidden Practice
Designing a system with yourself as a target user and evaluating it through self-usage is commonly considered "questionable" in HCI research. Despite this perception, many HCI researchers have engaged in extensive self-usage of their designs and found great value in the lessons learned. The problem: this practice is **underdocumented and underdiscussed** because researchers fear it will be seen as unscientific or biased.

### The Gap in HCI Methods
Two competing trends create tension:
1. **Increasing requirements** for formal evaluation in HCI publications
2. **Increasing difficulty** of conducting formal evaluations as systems become more complex, ubiquitous, and embedded in rich socio-technical contexts

Traditional lab studies and short-term field trials (3-6 weeks) struggle to capture:
- Long-term adoption patterns
- Real usage in authentic contexts
- Subtle, experiential qualities of systems

### Core Definition
**Autobiographical design** is defined as:
> "Design research drawing on extensive, genuine usage by those creating or building the system."

- **"Extensive"** = Measured in months or years, not days
- **"Genuine"** = Based on true needs of the researchers, not pretending to have expected user needs
- **NOT "dogfooding"** = Goes beyond testing for bugs; involves authentic, long-term use integrated into daily life

---

## T (Task): What the Paper Aims to Achieve

### Primary Goals
1. **Bring hidden practices to light** - Document autobiographical design as a legitimate HCI research method
2. **Articulate guidelines** - Provide best practices for when and how to use this method
3. **Establish validity** - Argue for the circumstances under which autobiographical design is appropriate and rigorous
4. **Support researchers** - Help others use the method effectively and help reviewers evaluate such work

### Research Questions Explored
- What practices do HCI experts use when engaging in autobiographical design?
- What types of knowledge are possible vs. impossible to achieve through self-usage?
- What ethical dimensions arise, and how should they be addressed?

### Key Findings: Practices

#### 1. Autobiographical Design Supports Fast Tinkering
- Tight integration of design cycles with real usage
- Immediate response to both formalizable aspects AND intuitions/feelings
- Shortened feedback cycle compared to traditional user studies
- **Quote:** *"Any design process benefits from having an end user on the team...they are right there, you can just ask them."*

#### 2. Requires Real, Working Systems
- Must build end-to-end systems that actually work (not just prototypes)
- Sometimes simple versions suffice; sometimes complexity is required from the start
- Technical/infrastructure hurdles can kill autobiographical design projects

#### 3. Best with Genuine Needs
- Respondents strongly advocated: **only do autobiographical design when you have real needs**
- Faking needs = limited understanding
- Best when the "builder" is the person with the need
- **Quote:** *"Genuine usage is really valuable because it causes you to use the system and try to integrate it with other parts of your life in practice in ways that you wouldn't tend to if you were just trying to test it."*

#### 4. Leverages Long-Term Usage
- Almost all respondents used systems for **over a year**
- Uncovers effects beyond novelty
- Captures how practices evolve over time
- **Quote:** *"If you want very very long term evaluations, that is extremely difficult to do...you're just not going to get that kind of data from someone who just signed up for the process."*

#### 5. Record-Keeping is Unusual (But Shouldn't Be)
- Most respondents kept NO formal records of self-use
- Only Erickson kept disciplined notes and logging
- Authors recommend: document needs, features used, design changes, and rationale

### Key Findings: What Can Be Learned

#### What Autobiographical Design CAN Do:
1. **Reveal "Big Effects"** - Major things that make or break a system
2. **Show genuine vs. discretionary needs** - What actually matters in practice
3. **Enable surprise and discovery** - Usage patterns that violate design expectations
4. **Provide detailed, experiential understanding** - Subtle insights unavailable through other methods
5. **Support early innovation** - Best for exploratory systems in new design niches
6. **Enable reflection-in-action** - Iterative design based on felt experience

#### What Autobiographical Design CANNOT Do:
1. **Prove generalizability** - Cannot establish that results apply to broader users
2. **Replace broader user testing** - Other users may appropriate technology unexpectedly
3. **Work for established problems** - Less useful when replacing existing systems

### Key Findings: Ethics

#### Tensions and Struggles
- Researchers feel tension between personal interest and professional ethics
- Concerns about "selfishness" and "bias"
- Need for self-vigilance to recognize emotional investment

#### Positive Ethical Dimensions
- **"In harm's way"** - Personal stake creates responsibility
- Real consequences make designers more careful
- Intimate confrontation with design decisions
- **Quote:** *"You are confronted with your design decisions in a very intimate way when you use an API you designed...you really really care about getting it to work right."*

#### Self-Editing in Publications
- Many researchers hide or downplay autobiographical aspects in papers
- Fear of reviewer rejection
- Result: Important parts of research remain unreported
- **Authors argue for full disclosure** - Be explicit about commitments and motivations

## R (Result): Main Contributions and Implications for Chris's Thesis

### Main Contributions

1. **Legitimized the Method** - Articulated autobiographical design as a valid HCI research approach
2. **Defined Quality Hallmarks:**
   - Extensive genuine use (months/years)
   - Surprises in usage that lead to rethinking
   - Improvements driven by documented incidents
   - Careful articulation of experiential impacts
3. **Provided Practical Guidelines** - How to do it well and how to evaluate it
4. **Opened Discussion** - Encouraged community to report and learn from these practices

### Guidelines for Doing Autobiographical Design Well

| Guideline | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Have genuine needs | Don't fake the motivation; real problems drive real insights |
| Build working systems | Must be functional enough for authentic daily use |
| Commit long-term | Months to years, not days or weeks |
| Keep records | Document needs, usage patterns, design changes, rationale |
| Embrace tinkering | Use tight feedback loops between design and use |
| Accept limitations | Can't prove generalizability; may need complementary methods |
| Be transparent | Explicitly report your commitments and motivations |
| Seek outside perspectives | Colleagues, family, "devil's advocates" can provide balance |

### When Autobiographical Design is Most Valid

✅ **Appropriate when:**
- Exploring a new design space with no established solutions
- Long-term understanding is needed
- Genuine personal need exists
- System can be built end-to-end quickly
- Seeking experiential, nuanced understanding

❌ **Less appropriate when:**
- Replacing existing systems with established use patterns
- Primary goal is proving generalizability
- No genuine personal need exists
- Infrastructure/technical hurdles are prohibitive

## Application to Chris's Self-Study Thesis

### Why This Paper is Relevant

Autobiographical design provides **methodological legitimacy** for Chris's self-study approach. If Chris is studying their own practices, behaviors, or experiences with a system they design/build, this paper offers:

1. **Academic grounding** - Self-study is not "cheating" if done rigorously
2. **Quality criteria** - Clear hallmarks of what makes self-usage research valid
3. **Reporting strategies** - How to present self-study honestly to reviewers

### Practical Recommendations for Chris

1. **Document Everything**
   - Keep a research journal/log of usage
   - Track design decisions and rationale
   - Record surprises and changes in practice
   - Consider automated logging of system usage

2. **Establish Genuine Need**
   - Design for a real problem Chris actually has
   - Don't pretend to have user needs that aren't authentic
   - Be explicit about personal motivations

3. **Commit to Long-Term Study**
   - Plan for months, not weeks
   - Track how practices evolve over time
   - Look beyond novelty effects

4. **Embrace Subjectivity While Maintaining Rigor**
   - Acknowledge bias rather than pretending objectivity
   - Use "careful, critical reflection" as the standard of rigor
   - Articulate experiential impacts in detail

5. **Complement with Other Perspectives**
   - Consider outsider critiques (advisors, peers)
   - If possible, eventually test with other users
   - But don't feel obligated to "validate" with broader studies

6. **Report Transparently**
   - Be explicit about self-study in publications
   - Describe commitments and motivations openly
   - Cite Neustaedter & Sengers to establish methodological legitimacy

### Potential Thesis Framing

Chris could position their self-study as:
> "This thesis employs autobiographical design methodology (Neustaedter & Sengers, 2012), drawing on extensive, genuine usage by the researcher to explore [design space]. Over [X months/years], I designed, built, and used [system] to understand [research question]. Following best practices for autobiographical design, I documented [practices, surprises, changes], maintained critical reflection on my own biases, and [complementary methods if any]."
